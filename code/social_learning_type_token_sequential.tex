\section{Materials}\label{materials}

\subsection{Faces}\label{faces}

We selected the stimuli from the 2,222 annotated face images in
\citep{Bainbridge2013}. For each gender and valency, we selected 4
(sets) \(\times\) 3 (actions per set) \(\times\) 6 (actions) \(\times\)
3 (occurrences per set) = 216 faces per gender.

We selected photographs of non-famous faces with an image quality rating
of at least 3.5, an estimated age between 20 and 60 years, and
memorability (operationalized as \citep{ZhangMueller2005} version of
\(A^\prime\)) between the 25\% and 85\% percentiles This yielded 278 and
380 photoes for female and male faces, respectively. I then randomly
selected the required number of faces from this initial set, with 216
faces being chosen for each gender.

The distribution of various face properties is shown in Figure
\ref{fig:plot-face-attributes-nonfamous}.

\section{Design and counterbalancing}\label{design-and-counterbalancing}

In the experiment, a traveler observed a number of actions in each city
he visits. Hereafter, I refer to a ``block'' of trials as all actions
occurring within a city, while trials are single occurrences of an
action.

Each block consisted of a behavior performed 6 times by the same
individual, one performed once by 6 different individuals each, and a
behavior performed once by one individual. To ensure that responses were
not based on prior estimates of the prevalence of individual actions,
the behaviors were counterbalanced across participants.

Specifically, the 12 actions per valence were grouped into four sets of
three actions. I then created three assignment conditions such that the
roles of actions within blocks (high type-frequency, low
type-frequency/high token-frequency, single occurrence) were
counterbalanced across participants (see Table
\ref{tab:counterbalancing-frequency-conditions-print2}).

The role of the actions within the blocks was counterbalanced within
participants. To maintain a reasonable experiment duration, the role of
valence was counterbalanced across participants, with each participant
seeing only one valence.

Specifically, all three assignment conditions were administered to all
participants. Each set of four actions thus occurred in three blocks, so
that each action was seen once as high type-frequency, low
type-frequency/high token-frequency and single occurrence action. The
faces and place names associated with each action differed across the
block. (I used the faces and placenames associated with the high
type-frequency action in the original table.) This design thus yielded
12 blocks in total that appeared in random order. (In terms of testable
programming, these blocks were implemented as randomblocks.)

For each participant, half of the blocks used female face and half used
male faces. The correspondence between sets and genders was
counterbalanced across participants. Specifically, for half of the
participants, Sets 1 and 2 were presented with male faces and Sets 3 and
4 with female faces. For the remaining participants, this assignment was
reversed.

This resulted in the 2 (gender/set correspondence) \(\times\) 3
(valence) = \(6\) participant groups shown in Table
\ref{tab:counterbalancing-gender-print2}

In each block, actions were presented in a random order, with the
constraint that all three types of actions (high type-frequency, low
type-frequency/high token-frequency, single occurrence) appeared in the
last three trials.

\section{Procedure}\label{procedure}

\subsection{Instructions}\label{instructions}

Participants were first told a cover story, where a student called Noah
took a gap year to visit different cities. He stayed 13 days in each
city and witnessed various interesting behaviors.

Participants were then instructed to estimate how widespread and morally
acceptable the events in each city were, using a 4-point scale.

Participants were also encouraged to use their gut reaction.

The specific instructions are shown in Table
\ref{tab:print-instructions2}.

\subsection{Attention check}\label{attention-check}

Following this, participants completed an attention check where they had
to select the name of the traveller from three options (Noah, Moses,
Fred). Only those who passed the attention check proceeded to the
experiment.

\subsection{Block structure}\label{block-structure}

Each trial began with a screen informing participants that Noah was
about to visit a new city, and that participants were about to read
about Noah's visit in . Participants had to acknowledge this screen by
pressing a button.

Then they were shown 13 trials, each involving three distinct actions (6
high type-frequency action trials, 6 low type-frequency/high
token-frequency action trials, and one single occurrence action trial).
Each trial featured a header indicating the day and the city name (e.g.,
``Day 2 in Cluakpus), a face, and a description of the action
(e.g.,''Noah went to the cinema and saw this person skip the queue'').
While the trials were presented in random order, all three types of
actions appear in the last three trials. Trials lasted 4500 ms, with a
500 ms intertrial-interval.

Finally, participants had to estimate the prevalence of each actions
(i.e., ``How likely are people in to \emph{}?''), its third-person
acceptability (i.e., Would \emph{people in } consider it morally
acceptable to \emph{}?``), and its first-person acceptability
(i.e.,''Would \emph{you} consider it morally acceptable to
\emph{}?{\kern0pt}``; all emphases in original). All question were
answered on a four point scale. In each trial, I first queried the high
type-frequency action, then the low type-frequency action, and finally
the single occurrence action.

In the pilot experiment with students, I also included the Short Dark
Triad (SD3) questionnaire \cite{Jones2014}, which was removed in the
main experiment.
